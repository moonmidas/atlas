## 🎯 The Opportunity

Fractional Supply Chain Ops services provide strategic supply chain leadership to product companies that have outgrown ad-hoc logistics but aren't ready for a full-time VP Supply Chain. These businesses typically have $5M-$50M in revenue, are managing physical products, and are experiencing sourcing challenges, inventory issues, or logistics complexity. They need supplier management, inventory optimization, and operational infrastructure—but can't justify $150K-$250K for a full-time executive.

A Fractional Supply Chain leader works 2-3 days per week, building the infrastructure for scalable operations while driving immediate cost and efficiency improvements.

**Market Size:** 300,000+ product companies needing supply chain leadership  
**Target:** CEOs and COOs of DTC, CPG, and product companies  
**Examples:** Supply chain consultancies, boutique operations firms, individual practitioners

## 💬 Objection Handling

**Do not we just need a logistics coordinator?**
> A logistics coordinator handles day-to-day shipments and tracking. A supply chain leader optimizes your entire network: supplier relationships, inventory strategy, demand planning, and cost structure. Most of my clients have both—a coordinator for execution and fractional leadership for strategy and optimization.

**How quickly can you deliver results?**
> Some improvements are immediate—supplier negotiations, freight rate optimization, process quick wins. Inventory optimization and demand planning improvements typically show results within 90 days as planning cycles complete. I focus on quick wins in the first 30 days to build momentum while working on longer-term structural improvements.

**What if we do not have supply chain data?**
> That is common in growth-stage companies. The first phase often includes setting up data collection and visibility—implementing tracking, establishing KPIs, and building reporting. You cannot optimize what you cannot see. I help build the data foundation as a prerequisite to optimization.

**How do we measure supply chain success?**
> Primary metrics include: inventory turns, stockout rate, forecast accuracy, total supply chain cost as % of revenue, and perfect order rate. Secondary metrics include supplier performance, warehouse efficiency, and cash conversion cycle. We establish baselines in month 1 and track improvements monthly.

**We have always done it this way and it works.**
> If it is truly working—low stockouts, optimized inventory, happy customers, controlled costs—then you may not need me. But most growth-stage companies I work with have outgrown their processes. What worked at $1M creates chaos at $10M. The question is whether your supply chain can support your growth goals, not whether it worked in the past.
