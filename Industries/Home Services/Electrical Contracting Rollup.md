# Electrical Contracting Rollup

**Category:** Home Services  
**Sub-Category:** Electrical Contractor Rollups  
**Entry Cost:** $3M-$20M  
**Timeline:** 12-24 months  
**Revenue Model:** Buy-and-build, platform value creation

---

## 🎯 The Opportunity

The electrical contracting industry represents a $200B+ market in the US with over 70,000 licensed contractors, yet remains highly fragmented with the top 50 companies holding less than 15% market share. Most are owner-operated with 1-10 employees, run by electricians who started their own business after getting their master license. The industry is experiencing tailwinds from electrification trends (EV charging, solar, heat pumps), aging infrastructure requiring updates, and code changes driving replacement demand.

Electrical work commands premium pricing due to safety requirements and licensing barriers. Platform opportunities include: (1) residential service work with recurring maintenance potential, (2) commercial maintenance contracts, (3) EV charging installation (fast-growing segment), (4) generator installation (increasingly popular), and (5) smart home integration. The industry has higher barriers to entry than many trades due to the master electrician requirement and safety liability.

**Market Size:** $200B+ US electrical contracting market  
**Target:** Owner-operated electrical contractors with $1M-$5M revenue  
**Examples:** Power Home Remodeling (electrical division), Mister Sparky franchises, regional rollups

## 🔴 Top 15 Pain Points

### 1. Master License Dependency ⭐ #1 PAIN
**Pain:** In most states, electrical work must be supervised by a master electrician. If the owner has the only master license and wants to retire, the business is essentially worthless. Creates massive key person risk.
**Current Solution:** Hire another master (expensive and hard to find), stay working forever, or close
**Cost of Pain:** 50-70% discount on business value, owner trapped in business
**Your Solution:** Platform maintains master licensing across all jurisdictions, removes key person risk, enables exit
**Pricing:** Removing key person risk adds 1-2x to exit multiple = $500K-$2M in value

**Discovery Questions:**
- "Who holds the master electrician license for your company?"
- "Do you have other master electricians on staff?"
- "What would happen to the business if you couldn't work for 6 months?"

---

### 2. Skilled Electrician Shortage
**Pain:** Electricians require 4-5 year apprenticeships. Trade school enrollment is down. Experienced journeymen command $40-$60/hour. Training new apprentices is time-consuming and expensive.
**Current Solution:** Pay above-market wages, work overtime, take on apprentices selectively
**Cost of Pain:** $75K-$300K annually in overtime, recruitment, and lost revenue from capacity constraints
**Your Solution:** Platform apprenticeship programs, recruiting pipeline, training infrastructure, career ladders
**Pricing:** Solving labor constraints enables 20-25% revenue growth = $200K-$1M annually

**Discovery Questions:**
- "How many electrician positions are you trying to fill right now?"
- "What do you pay for experienced journeymen vs. apprentices?"
- "Do you have a formal apprenticeship program?"

---

### 3. Code Compliance & Liability
**Pain:** NEC (National Electrical Code) updates every 3 years. State and local amendments vary. Permitting requirements are complex. One mistake can cause fire, injury, or death—massive liability exposure.
**Current Solution:** Continuing education classes, hope inspectors are reasonable, carry high insurance
**Cost of Pain:** $25K-$100K annually in insurance, compliance costs, potential lawsuits
**Your Solution:** Platform compliance management, standardized procedures, liability pooling, best practice sharing
**Pricing:** Risk mitigation + operational efficiency = $30K-$100K annual value

**Discovery Questions:**
- "How do you keep up with code changes and continuing education?"
- "Have you ever had a failed inspection or liability issue?"
- "What does your liability insurance cost annually?"

---

### 4. Safety & Workers' Comp
**Pain:** Electrical work is dangerous. Arc flash, falls, shock hazards. Workers' comp rates are 8-15% of payroll for electricians. One serious injury affects rates for years.
**Current Solution:** Safety training, PPE requirements, hope for the best
**Cost of Pain:** $50K-$200K annually in insurance premiums, lost time, OSHA compliance
**Your Solution:** Platform safety programs, claims management, experience mod improvement, group purchasing
**Pricing:** Reducing workers' comp costs 20% = $40K-$120K annually

**Discovery Questions:**
- "What are your workers' comp rates per $100 of payroll?"
- "Have you had any serious injuries or OSHA issues?"
- "What safety training do you provide?"

---

### 5. Project Estimating Complexity
**Pain:** Electrical jobs are complex to estimate—materials, labor hours, permit costs, unknown conditions in walls. Estimates are often wrong, leading to margin erosion or lost bids.
**Current Solution:** Experience-based estimating, occasionally job cost afterward, hope averages work
**Cost of Pain:** 10-15% margin variance = $100K-$500K in lost profit or missed revenue
**Your Solution:** Platform estimating tools, historical databases, standardized pricing, takeoff software
**Pricing:** Improving estimate accuracy 300-500 bps = $100K-$500K annually

**Discovery Questions:**
- "How do you currently create estimates for electrical jobs?"
- "What percentage of estimates are accurate within 10%?"
- "Do you track job costing and compare to estimates?"

---

### 6. Material Procurement & Supply Chain
**Pain:** Copper and aluminum prices are volatile. Lead times on panels and equipment are 8-16 weeks. Buying retail from supply houses kills margins. No purchasing power.
**Current Solution:** Pay retail prices, carry inventory, delay projects for materials
**Cost of Pain:** 5-10% premium on materials vs. wholesale = $50K-$200K annually
**Your Solution:** Platform purchasing power, vendor relationships, inventory management, price hedging
**Pricing:** Improved material margins + working capital efficiency = $75K-$300K annually

**Discovery Questions:**
- "Where do you source your electrical materials?"
- "Do you have preferred pricing or volume discounts?"
- "How have supply chain issues affected your business?"

---

### 7. Residential vs. Commercial Imbalance
**Pain:** Over-concentration in one segment creates risk. Residential service is profitable but competitive; commercial has longer sales cycles and receivables but steadier work.
**Current Solution:** Take what comes, occasionally bid commercial, hope for the best
**Cost of Pain:** Concentration risk, missed diversification worth $150K-$500K annually
**Your Solution:** Balanced portfolio strategy, commercial capabilities, platform estimating resources
**Pricing:** Optimal service mix improves stability and growth 15-20%

**Discovery Questions:**
- "What's your split between residential service, commercial, and new construction?"
- "Do you prefer service work or project work? Why?"
- "How do you handle estimating for larger commercial jobs?"

**Cost of Pain:** 10-15% margin leakage on subcontracted work = $40K-$200K annually

**Pricing:** Bringing work in-house improves margins 8-12% = $30K-$150K annually

**Discovery Questions:**

## 🚨 Red Flags

**Avoid:**
- ❌ Owner is the only master electrician (insurmountable key person risk)
- ❌ Heavy new construction concentration (>50% of revenue)
- ❌ Unresolved safety violations or OSHA issues
- ❌ Workers' comp experience mod >1.2
- ❌ Significant litigation or liability claims

**Best Prospects:**
- Service-focused (vs. new construction)
- $1M-$5M revenue with positive EBITDA

## 💬 Objection Handling

**"I can't sell because I'm the only master electrician"**
> "That's actually the exact reason many electrical contractors work with us. We solve the master license problem as part of the transaction. We'll recruit and sponsor a master electrician to take over supervisory responsibility, ensuring business continuity. This allows you to exit while preserving value for your employees and customers. The alternative—waiting until you're forced to close—is much worse. Can I show you how we handled this for [Previous Acquisition]?"

**"My customers expect to deal with me personally"**
> "That's a common concern, and it actually speaks to the quality reputation you've built. Here's what we've learned: customers care about consistent, quality service more than which specific electrician shows up. We keep your brand, your team, and your phone number. What changes is behind the scenes—better scheduling, faster response times, 24/7 availability. Most customers never notice the ownership change, but they do notice improved service."

**"I don't want to work for someone else after selling"**
> "I completely understand. Most of our sellers stay involved for 6-12 months during transition—but as a consultant, not an employee. You set your hours, choose your involvement level, and help ensure continuity. After that, you're free to retire, start a new venture, or do whatever you want. The goal is a dignified exit on your terms. What would your ideal transition look like?"

**"I've heard horror stories about private equity"**
> "There are certainly bad actors out there. The difference is: we're operators first, investors second. We've actually run electrical contracting businesses. We know the challenges you face. Our approach is to invest in growth—better tools, training, marketing—not to cut costs and flip the business. We hold companies 7-10 years on average. Does that sound different from what you've heard?"

**"I'm worried about my employees' jobs"**
> "That's exactly the kind of owner we want to work with. Here's our commitment: every employee who wants to stay keeps their job, gets better benefits, and has a path for career growth. We invest in training, safety, and equipment. Many of our acquired companies grow 20-30% in the first year, creating more opportunities, not fewer. Would you like to talk to some employees who've been through this?"
